Dan Snyder Era to Present Era

The Lowest of the Lows for Professional Football in D.C. ... but with a Ray of Hope for the Future

Dan Snyder owned the team for 24 years, yet his tenure featured only six playoff appearances, five winning seasons, and two playoff wins.

Washington had a carousel of coaches and quarterbacks during the Snyder Era, marking an era of instability and dwelling in the basement of the NFC East.

Despite Snyder's incompetency, there were some bright spots in the franchise that unforunately did not pan out due to unforeseen circumstances.

The first was All-Pro safety Sean Taylor, who the Redskins drafted fifth overall from the University of Miami.

Taylor made the NFL's All-Pro Second Team at the young age of 24, and flashed the potential to become one of the great safeties in league history.

His potential would sadly never be realized, however, as Taylor was fatally shot during a home invasion in Miami.

The murder of Taylor remains one of the greatest tragedies in franchise history, and continues to be one of the NFL's most discussed "what if?" stories.

Another sign of hope for the Redskins was Robert Griffin III. Washington drafted the Heisman Trophy winner second overall in the 2012 Draft.

In 2012, Griffin propelled the Redskins to a top-five scoring offense and their most exciting season in years. Griffin was named AP Offensive Rookie of the Year and Washington finished 10-6.

The Redskins hosted the Seahawks in the Wild Card round in front of a crowd of over 84,000 at FedEx Field. Wahington jumped out to a 14-0 lead, then disaster struck.

After Washington surrendered 21 unasnwered points, Griffin, playing through a knee injury, chased down a bad snap. Griffin then fell to the ground clutching his knee and couldn't get back up.

The young franchise quarterback was diagnosed with a torn ACL, LCL, and meniscus in his right knee, permanently derailing his career.

Washington hasn't reached those offensive heights or double-digit wins in a season since, progressing through numerous starting quarterbacks and head coaches.

In 2020, following years of controversy surrounding the term "Redskins", the franchise temporarily changed its name to the "Washington Football Team".

After two years of searching for a permanent name change, the franchise decided on "Washington Commanders", to recognize the military ties of the city of Washington, D.C.

The rebrand received mixed reviews and the verdict is still out on whether the fanbase generally likes or dislikes the new identity (it seems to be leaning more towards dislike).

In 2023, the fanbase received the best news they'd heard in years: Dan Snyder was discussing selling the team.

Snyder began negotiations in April and the deal to sell the franchise to a group led by Josh Harris was unananimously approved by NFL owners on July 20, 2023.

After a disappointing 2023 season, the Commanders had the most active offseason of any team, hiring new head coach Dan Quinn and starting fresh with a new coaching staff, drafting potential franchise cornerstone Jayden Daniels second overall, and overhauling the roster.

In only a year's time, Josh Harris gave fans more to believe in than Snyder had in years.

Under Harris' ownership, one of the most passionate fanbases in the NFL now has hope that the franchise will return professional football in Washington, D.C. to its former glory.
